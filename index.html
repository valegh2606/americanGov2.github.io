The Legislative Branch

The Branches of Government

The Federal Government is made up of three different branches to ensure a separation of powers. They are called the legislative, executive, and judicial branches of government. Their powers are vested by the U.S. Constitution in the Congress, the President, and the Federal courts, respectively.

The Legislative Branch

The legislative branch is also known as Congress, which is made up by the House and the senate. After the founding founders drafted the constitution, they debated over the types of legislatures it would have and eventually settled on having both equal representation and a proportional representation. Its power is to make laws, declare wars, regulate interstate and foreign commerce, and control taxing and spending policies. It is the only branch that is elected directly by the people and is the only part of government that balances between power of the nation and the individual state.

How Does Congress Work?

The lower house, the house of representatives, is led by the speaker of the house who initiates tax laws, spending bills, and initiates impeachment of the president or other government officials. The upper house, the Senate, has the exclusive power to approve presidential appointments and treaties. Congress uses the checks and balances system to prevent one branch from gaining too much power. Writing and passing bills is Congress’s main power for the federal government.

Bills

After a bill originates in either the house or senate, it must pass through a series of committees, amendments and floor debates. After a vote, both houses must agree with the bill and if one makes changes, it needs to go back to the original house for another vote. The vice president oversees the senate but in the event of a draw, the vice may vote to break the tie. The House and Senate must agree and vote on the exact same bill before it can move on. If there is no agreement, the bill must be reintroduced, however, if it passes it may go to the president's desk for approval. If the president vetoes the bill, Congress may override it with a 2/3 majority vote from both houses. If approved, the president may sign it into law.

Membership of the House

The House of Representatives has 435 members. The House seats must be divided among the states by population. Each state is entitled to at least one seat in the House of Representatives.

Qualifications

  • At least 25 years old
  • A citizen of the United States for at least 7 years
  • A legal resident of the state they represent

Term of Office

Representatives are elected for two-year terms with elections being held in November of even-numbered years.

Membership of the Senate

The Senate “shall be composed of two senators from each state” according to the Constitution. Thus, today’s Senate includes 100 members.

Qualifications

  • At least 30 years old
  • A citizen of the United States for at least 9 years
  • A legal resident of the state they represent

Term of Office

The Constitution provides for Senate continuity by giving senators six-year terms and providing that only 1/3 run for reelection every two years.
